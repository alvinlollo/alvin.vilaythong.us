Childrens Online Privacy code analysis

3 May 2026·452 words·3 mins·
Author
Alvin Vilaythong

Australia is going be releasing law to “protect” children online by introducing the social media ban on December 2025. Now a new Exposure Draft has been proposed to the whole of Australia named the Children’s Online Privacy Code which is attempted to replicate the UK’s Appropriate Design Code. The OAIC states that the code was created to help parents and children manage privacy to the child’s best interests. But perhaps this is a Trojan horse disguised as mass surveillance? |

The Children’s Online Privacy Code has been enforced to “all services a child may access”. This is a incredibly broad term that has been created to mention the entirety of the internet. This means all platforms mus adopt and comply with the new Children’s Online Privacy Code. This code explicitly excludes Telecommunications service providers who track every place your phone goes down the the centimetre with 5G and triangulation. This means they can track your routine, who you interact with, where your house is, and who has been near a crime. This is helpful for law enforcement but this info can be easily abused and sold to other companies. If police officers have someone they wish to stalk, the information is right there to do so. Would you trust a massive company to hold your information, unencrypted and unsecured? The new code also tells companies to take reasonable steps to determine if a user is a child. Imagine having to input your birthday or complete a face scan to every site you visit even without an account, this would strip the internet of its anominity and become a nuisance.

On the other hand this new law requires companies to give privacy control to users who are underage, The Children’s Online Privacy Code requires services to set all settings to the highest privacy settings, not collect unnecessary information without explicit consent (especially marketing), explain all information collection, provide a way for parents to get notified of privacy changes, immediate unrecoverable data deletion on the settings page or some UI, notification of any data breaches, and handling inquires within 30 days. This seems like a benefit for digital sovereignty and information handling. And brings awareness to data collection and security breaches. This law is a amazing privacy management win.

If you prefer to stay anonymous online for your personal safety then this law is not for you, whereas if you are privacy oriented this law will help you manage your privacy. This law is reducing anominity and increasing privacy. Do you think this is a fair trade? I personally think that this is not a good law as services must invest time and money into determining if a user is underage and reduces anominity.
